Overlap-Aware Chunking, Briefly

Splitting a long document into smaller pieces for retrieval sounds simple until a relevant sentence lands exactly on a chunk boundary. A naive splitter cuts the sentence in half, and whichever half a search matches loses the context the other half carried, so the match scores lower than it should even though the passage was genuinely relevant to the query that found it.

An overlap-aware splitter fixes this by repeating a short tail of each chunk at the start of the next one, so a boundary sentence still has enough surrounding context in at least one of the two chunks it touches. The overlap costs a little storage and a little redundant text, and buys back nearly all of the recall a naive splitter would have thrown away for free.

None of this is exotic. It is the same trade-off a print typesetter makes with a running header repeating a chapter title on every page, so a reader who opens to any single page still knows roughly where they are without having to flip back to the start.
