# AgentM Auto-Organization

## Context

### Objective

The vault accumulates cruft as it grows: cold notes crowd the browse surface, most notes carry no links, and nothing cleans up on its own. **This design makes the vault maintain itself** â€” work artifacts tuck away at project close or after a year unused, memories fade on a stepped schedule and reach the archive only after five years of silence, new notes arrive linked to their neighbors, and a lint pass repairs the small rot. A shelved artifact stays in everyday search but leaves your browse eyeline; an archived memory answers only an explicit archive search; nothing is ever deleted, and none of it waits on your confirmation. We define the signals, the three actions, the machinery that runs them, and the meters that prove the vault is getting tidier.

### Background

You asked for this directly: cut down the cruft, and archive older notes so they are there when needed but out of the way when you browse agentm's memory. Today the engine scores every volatile note's decay, but that score only lowers the note's rank in search â€” the browse surface never gets cleaner. The measured baselines say the rest: 13.8% of notes carry an organic link, and three whole kinds hold 880 notes with no links at all.

Every mechanism this design needs already runs. The lifecycle layer already stamps every note's last genuine recall â€” the clock every schedule here reads. Dreaming walks the whole corpus weekly. It uses staged proposals, a revert log, and an auto-apply lane you ruled on for compression. The archive convention exists at every tier. Recall skips `_archive/` by default. The `--include-archive` flag reopens it. The capture design is on the same arc. It hands newly ingested batches to the linker.

The new work adds three focused pieces. These are an archive stage for dreaming, a write-time linker, and a lint engine. Each piece rides the shipped machinery. Building a second corpus walker duplicates the staging, confirmation, and revert plumbing of dreaming. Everything scheduled here runs as a dreaming stage.

This design is the memory-system design's own lifecycle tail, built out. That design names decay-to-archive and self-healing lint as designed-for; this child design is where they become real. It stands alone because the work spans two parents â€” the lifecycle belongs to the memory system, the weekly stages ride dreaming in the experience design â€” and because three plan-sized builds deserve their own document rather than a heavier amendment log. At landing, both parent designs gain an amendment pointing here.

## Design

### Overview

Auto-organization runs three actions over the vault, and this design is organized the same way. First, **linking at write time**: a new note arrives already connected â€” the engine finds its nearest neighbors and adds the links before the note settles in. Second, **tidying on age**: work artifacts leave the eyeline at project close or after a year of sitting unused, and memories fade on a stepped schedule until, after five years of silence, they reach the archive â€” where only an explicit archive search finds them. Third, **lint**: a pass that finds orphans, collapses duplicate notes, repairs broken links, discovers missing links between new content and the older notes it relates to, flags contradictions, and scores note quality â€” fixing and adding the safe things itself and surfacing the rest. Links are only ever added; the single removal case is a link that is already broken.

The machinery is the existing weekly dreaming pass â€” which also absorbs the inbox triage that used to wait for your command â€” plus one small write-time hook. Nothing is ever deleted: shelf and archive both mean move, every move auto-applies with a revert-log entry and a digest line, and two meters â€” organic connectivity and the browse-surface counts â€” tell you whether the vault is actually getting tidier. A sampled audit, a global mutation budget, and an anomaly breaker watch the automation itself.

### Infrastructure

Everything runs on the existing dreaming pipeline, the runner, and the save path. The new components are:

| Component | What it is |
|---|---|
| write-time linker | a post-save step: finds related notes via the vector index, adds wikilinks to the new note |
| tidying stage | a new dreaming stage: shelves artifacts unused for a year, archives memories silent for five |
| lint engine | orphan, broken-link, contradiction, and quality checks; runs on demand and weekly |
| link graph snapshot | a derived, device-local copy of the vault's typed-edge graph, rebuilt incrementally each cycle |
| dedup guard | a pre-write check: an arriving note that matches an existing one reinforces it instead of duplicating it |
| automation guards | the sampled higher-tier audit, the global mutation budget, and the anomaly breaker |
| backfill batches | link additions for today's unlinked notes, capped per cycle |

| Trigger | What runs |
|---|---|
| every save, and every ingest batch | the write-time linker |
| the weekly dreaming cycle | inbox triage, the tidying stage, dedup, lint, and the backfill batches |
| every write | the dedup guard |
| `/memory lint` | the lint engine, on demand |

The system makes four guarantees. Every shelf, archive, and link move auto-applies, lands in the revert log, and shows in the digest â€” none of it waits on you. The linker's additions are additive and capped. Judgment calls â€” contradictions, repairs that are not clearly safe â€” are surfaced, never acted on. A crashed cycle resumes where it left off.

### Detailed Design

#### The signals

Two clocks and two shipped signals drive every action. The lifecycle layer stamps a note's last genuine recall; this design retunes its rank curve from the original 30-day half-life to your stepped, years-long schedule â€” full strength for six months of silence, half to a year, an eighth to three years, a sixteenth to five. The retune ships shadow-first: for the first cycles both curves compute and the rank deltas log, and the new curve takes over only after the diff reads sane and the pinned retrieval eval holds. The same touch clock extends to artifacts, where any injection into a conversation counts as a touch. The `fingerprint` field every entry already carries supplies exact-duplicate detection, and the vector index supplies the near-duplicate signal. Durable notes, failure incidents, and decision paths are exempt, and always-load or pinned notes are never candidates for anything here. The vector index supplies nearest neighbors by similarity. The typed-edge graph supplies the link picture, persisted as a derived, device-local snapshot so cycle queries cost nothing. It shows who points at whom and who is an orphan.

#### Linking at write time

*Task 3 of 7 (the write-time linker itself) shipped 2026-07-18 â€” see the as-built note after the paragraph below for how its timing actually works. Tasks 4, 5, 6, and 7 (the weekly link-improvement sweep, the ingest-batch handoff, the backfill, and the connectivity meter) have since shipped too, each with its own as-built note. All 7 tasks of this part are now shipped â€” see the Document History entry below for the part-2 landing.*

When a note is saved, the linker asks the index for its nearest neighbors. It keeps the ones above a similarity floor. It adds up to three wikilinks to the new note under a short "Related" line. It runs after the save returns. It works the same way the embedding queue does, so saving stays fast. It edits only the note that just arrived. A reciprocal link on an older note is batched into the weekly cycle, capped and revert-logged. Ingest batches from the capture design arrive internally linked already. The linker adds their outward connections. The similarity floor and the three-link cap are calibration defaults. They are named in code and tuned from the connectivity meter.

**As built (task 3 â€” the write-time linker itself):** the linker is real and shipped, but "it runs after the save returns" is truer, and reads differently, than it first sounds. It is not synchronous on the save call's own return path. A version that queried the index and embedded the new note inline in `save.py` was tried first and caused a confirmed regression: it made every single save trigger a real BGE model load, and â€” on this machine â€” a live HuggingFace Hub network round-trip even with the model already cached. A follow-up fix forcing `HF_HUB_OFFLINE=1` was tried and also confirmed broken (offline mode fails to resolve an already-cached model from disk on this machine's sentence-transformers/huggingface_hub version, a known revision-resolution quirk). The shipped mechanism instead defers the actual linking to the *next drain of the embedding queue*: `save.py`'s post-save hook is now just `vec_index.enqueue(vault, rel_path, "upsert", text=embed_text, link=True)` â€” one new `link=True` flag on the same record the embedding queue already writes. `drain_queue()` applies `write_time_linker.apply()` right after computing that record's embedding for the ordinary vec-index upsert, reusing the same embedding rather than a second, save-time-only embed call. So the sentence above â€” "it works the same way the embedding queue does, so saving stays fast" â€” is honored exactly, and more literally than it first reads: the save call itself never touches the network or loads a model, but a newly saved note's "Related" links appear on the *next drain cycle* (the idle-time hook, a manual `python3 vec_index.py drain`, or a future `/memory reindex`) rather than before `save_entry()` returns. Applying the link also nudges task 2's persisted graph snapshot for just that one note (`graph_snapshot.rebuild(vault, paths=[rel_path])`), so the graph doesn't go stale waiting on the weekly full rebuild. The reciprocal-link case shipped with task 4 (its own as-built note appears further below), and the ingest-batch handoff shipped with task 5 (as-built note just below this one). The backfill (described below) shipped with task 6 â€” see its own as-built note just after that paragraph. The connectivity meter (task 7) shipped too â€” see its own as-built note under "The meters" section below.

**As built (task 5 â€” the ingest-batch handoff):** shipped exactly as the sentence above already described â€” the only task in this part that landed with no divergence from its own design text. A new `write_time_linker.same_ingest_batch(slug_a, slug_b)` matches `ingest.py`'s deterministic `<doc-slug>-chunk-<i>` slug formula: sibling chunks of the same document, and a chunk against its own parent document, both count as the same batch; everything else doesn't. Both linking surfaces call it â€” task 3's `apply()` at write time, and task 4's `_stage_link_improvement()` in the weekly sweep â€” skipping same-batch neighbors before they ever become a proposed link, since a batch already carries its reading-order nav and document backlink from ingest itself. One companion fix turned out to be necessary alongside the filter, not named in the design text: querying the vector index at exactly the three-link cap returned nothing but a chunk's own near-identical siblings, crowding every outward candidate out of the result window before the filter ever got a chance to run. Both surfaces now query with `_NEIGHBOR_QUERY_HEADROOM` (8) extra results beyond the cap â€” cheap on a sqlite-vec top-k query â€” so an outward candidate stays visible in the window the filter works over. Verified against a real ingest of capture part 2's own fixture (`scripts/fixtures/ingest/sample-article.md`, via `ingest.ingest()`) plus a planted older related note, asserting outward links land in both directions and zero intra-batch Related links appear (`scripts/test_dream.py::IngestBatchHandoffTests`).

The backfill clears the existing debt. Currently, 880 notes across three kinds carry no links at all. Each weekly cycle links a capped batch of them. The batch limit is 25 per cycle, matching compression's cap. This continues until the pool drains.

**As built (task 6 â€” the backfill):** shipped folded into the same `_stage_link_improvement()` rather than as a separate stage â€” the design's text above describes the outcome, not a mechanism, and a second stage would have duplicated the per-origin link flow task 4 already built. After the changed-notes loop runs, the stage pulls `graph_snapshot.orphans(vault_path)` (zero edges in either direction, freshly accurate because this same stage rebuilds the snapshot in full at its own start) and runs up to `_LINK_BACKFILL_BATCH_CAP` (25, matching `dream_confirm.py`'s existing compression cap, as the plan specifies) of them through `_process_origin()` â€” the identical exemption-gate, embed, neighbor-query, both-direction-merge flow task 4's changed-notes loop already uses, now a shared closure both loops call. Backfill proposals carry the same `stage="link_improvement"` as any other candidate â€” so `AUTO_APPLY_STAGES` and the revert log treat them identically â€” but `kind="backfill"` instead of `kind="link"`, a label that costs nothing structurally and exists purely so the digest can tell the two apart. The two batch caps are independent: a cycle can propose up to 25 changed-note links and up to 25 backfill links, not 25 shared between them. A persisted attempted-set (`_meta/link-backfill-state.json`, alongside task 4's own sweep cursor) rotates the pool across cycles: an orphan that found no qualifying neighbor this cycle is skipped on the next one rather than re-occupying one of the 25 batch slots every time, which would otherwise starve the rest of the pool from ever getting a turn. Once every orphan has had a turn, the pass resets and previously-unmatched notes get a fresh chance â€” some of them may qualify by then, since newer content keeps arriving. A note leaves the pool the ordinary way: once it picks up a link (as a backfill origin, or as some other note's neighbor), it's no longer an orphan and `graph_snapshot.orphans()` stops returning it. Verified with a 30-orphan fixture split into two mutually-similar-but-orthogonal groups of 25 and 5 (so the carry-over set is exact, not just approximately right): cycle 1 processes exactly the first 25, the remaining 5 carry to cycle 2, and re-running a cycle against notes already linked in a prior one doesn't reprocess them (`scripts/test_dream.py::LinkBackfillTests`).

#### Deduplication â€” reinforce, never repeat

The inbox shows the problem plainly: whole families of near-identical memories suffixed `_1`, `_2`, and up. They exist because the write path handles a name collision by suffixing instead of asking whether the content already exists, and because the two shipped cleanup mechanisms each have a hole â€” triage's merge works on pairs and only runs when you invoke it, and dreaming's dedup stage skips the inbox and still waits on confirmation. This design closes all three gaps by extending what exists rather than adding a third mechanism.

Prevention comes first. The dedup guard runs before any note is written: an exact `fingerprint` match or a strong near-match against the index means the arriving note reinforces the existing one â€” its occurrence count and `updated` stamp bump, and no new file appears. A suffix now only ever means genuinely different content sharing a title.

**As built (task 2 â€” the write-time dedup guard):** the guard shipped EXACT-fingerprint-match only; the "or a strong near-match against the index" half of the paragraph above was deliberately not built at write time. The plan's own Locked design call â€” fingerprint-exact collapses are deterministic, fuzzy merges need a model verdict, no exception either direction â€” rules out a synchronous near-match auto-reinforce at save time: it would discard the arriving note's real differences without the verdict the locked call requires, and computing the embedding a near-match needs would reintroduce the exact synchronous-model-load-per-save regression part 2's own as-built note already records fixing (see "Linking at write time" above). Near-duplicates write normally today; they are the weekly cluster pass's (task 3, upcoming) to own â€” this paragraph's "strong near-match" clause is designed-for at that layer, not this one.

The guard (`harness/skills/memory/scripts/dedup_guard.py`) hooks two write paths. `save_entry()` looks up `entry_meta.fingerprint` in the vec-index DB (task 1's column), then re-verifies the match against the candidate file's current live body before reinforcing â€” a stale index row, which lags the next drain by design, must never cause a reinforce against content that has since changed, so a match is never trusted from a stored value alone. `capture()` runs a direct frontmatter scan of `personal/_inbox/` (small by design; the index never carries inbox candidates). Both hold `vault_mutex` across the find-then-reinforce pair, so two concurrent identical writes can't both slip past the guard.

An adversarial review caught two gaps in an early version's match-target filtering. First, only a note carrying `status: active` (vault) or `status: inbox` (inbox candidates) is a legitimate reinforce target â€” a triage-expired tombstone, a `deleted`/`superseded` note, or a curated `personal/_always-load/` standing rule never absorbs a duplicate. The first version checked this only on the arriving note, so a re-capture of an already-expired thought would have reinforced its own tombstone and never re-entered triage, breaking `capture()`'s "a capture is never silently dropped" contract. Second, `capture()`'s reinforce path now refuses the match outright when the arriving capture carries `source_url` or `instructions` the matched candidate lacks â€” a link resend deduping into an earlier plain-text capture would otherwise silently drop the ingest-sweep trigger those fields carry.

`reinforce()` is the one mutation: it bumps the matched note's `occurrences` count (absent = 1, so a first reinforce sets 2) and stamps `updated` with today's date, patched in place â€” no new file, no body rewrite. `occurrences` joined `save.FRONTMATTER_FIELD_ORDER` as a new optional field, patch-only: an ordinary write never emits it, only a reinforce ever does. `save_entry()`'s existing same-slug-collision path is untouched â€” the guard sits upstream of it, and a genuine slug collision with different content still raises `FileExistsError` exactly as before.

The document-ingest path (`ingest()`) short-circuits on a whole-document dedup: a duplicate document returns the existing note with `deduplicated=True` and writes zero chunks, rather than writing a chunk family that would dangle-link to a document note that never materialized (the bug an earlier version had). Chunk-level dedup within a document threads the same `dedup_info` signal through, so a mid-sequence rollback never unlinks a note the guard had already reinforced.

Both MCP tools are truthful about it now â€” an earlier version was not, review-caught. `memory_append` reports the real `deduplicated` flag and, on a reinforce, the existing note's own slug rather than the phantom slug the request asked for; `memory_capture` surfaces `CaptureResult.deduplicated` the same way. See [Memory MCP tools](Memory-MCP-Tools) for the response shape.

The cure runs weekly, inside dreaming, as one pass. Inbox triage folds into the cycle as a stage: it stops waiting for your command, and the inbox joins the same walk as the rest of the corpus. The two already share their staging and merge machinery underneath â€” this fold closes a historical split, and `/memory inbox` stays as the on-demand door into the same engine, alongside `/memory lint`. Dedup itself becomes cluster-aware: a whole suffix family collapses into the canonical earliest note in one disposition. A collapse is free when fingerprints match exactly â€” a suffix family is one note repeated. A fuzzy merge, similar but not identical, needs a cheap-model verdict before it applies, and a verdict the judge is unsure of joins the needs-your-eye list. Every collapse keeps the surviving note's content, marks the copies superseded â€” they then follow the normal tidying lanes â€” and auto-applies, capped, revert-logged, and digest-reported. Dreaming's corpus dedup was confirm-gated by its own standing ruling; approving this design is the fresh ruling that extends auto-apply to it, under these bounds.

**As built (task 3 â€” the cluster-aware weekly pass):** shipped as a two-pass scan inside `_build_merge_and_promote_clusters`, which now returns a `ClusterScan` dataclass (`collapse_proposals`, `merge_proposals`, `promote_clusters`, `needs_your_eye`) rather than the old 2-tuple. One boundary matters here that this paragraph's own opening sentence doesn't yet have: "inside dreaming" is task 4's job, shipped separately â€” see its own as-built note below â€” this task only rebuilt `inbox_triage.py`'s own engine, which at the time this task shipped still ran exactly where it always had, on demand via `/memory inbox` or the CLI, not yet folded into `run_dream()`'s weekly walk. Pass 1 buckets the inbox pool by `dedup_guard.live_content_fingerprint` â€” the same live-recomputed hash the write-time guard (task 2) matches on â€” and a bucket of two or more is a suffix family, content-identical modulo formatting. The whole family collapses into the canonical EARLIEST note (by `created` frontmatter, path-order tiebreak for the legacy backlog shape with no `created` at all) in one disposition, a new `inbox_collapse` stage (`kind="collapse"`): every copy is marked `status: superseded` + `supersedes: <canonical>` â€” never deleted, so part 1's tidying lanes still pick the copies up later â€” and the survivor's own content is left untouched, since a copy carries nothing new to absorb. `inbox_collapse` joined `ALL_AUTO_APPLY_STAGES` alongside `inbox_promote`/`inbox_merge`, on the plan's own Locked design call ("Fingerprint-exact collapses are deterministic; fuzzy merges need a model verdict â€” no exception either direction"): membership in an exact family is a content-hash fact, not a judgment call, so it needs no verdict to auto-apply.

Pass 2 runs `dream._stage_dedup` (the same `difflib` comparison and threshold as always) over whatever the inbox pool has left once exact families are claimed. A hub matched by two or more others still becomes a PROMOTE cluster, untouched by this task â€” promotion is triage's recurrence mechanic, not dedup. A hub matched by exactly one other is now a FUZZY merge candidate, and this is where this section's "needs a cheap-model verdict" clause actually lands: a new `judge_fuzzy_merge(hub_body, other_body)` seam returns "yes" / "no" / "unsure", gated behind `dream.cheap_model_tier_available()` â€” the same seam part 2's link-improvement sweep (task 4) introduced, and it still always returns `False` today, so `judge_fuzzy_merge` is unreachable in production and raises rather than guessing if it's ever called without the tier. "Yes" emits dream's own merge-into-earlier / mark-later-superseded mutations verbatim; "no" leaves both notes exactly as they are; "unsure" â€” or the tier being unavailable, which is every run today â€” routes the pair to the needs-your-eye list instead. **The behavior this replaces is gone on purpose:** before this task, a pairwise difflib match auto-applied as a merge with no verdict at all; that path no longer exists, per the plan's Locked design call ruling out any exception either direction.

Separately, the occurrence-promote threshold now compares against `max(mining_occurrences, occurrences)` rather than `mining_occurrences` alone, unifying reflect's own miner counter with task 2's write-time reinforce counter â€” a note reinforced enough times via the write-time guard now promotes exactly as reliably as one reflect mined repeatedly.

Questionable cases stay put. A pair or family in the ambiguous band is left in the inbox untouched and flagged instead: a needs-your-eye list on the console, a line in the digest, and a count in the morning brief. You resolve those whenever you like.

A flagged pair's paths are claimed the moment it lands on the list, exempting it from that same run's occurrence-based promote and expire passes â€” an ambiguous pair sitting right at its TTL boundary can't expire in the very cycle that flagged it.

**As built (task 5 of 9 â€” the console and morning-brief surfaces):** the two remaining surfaces this paragraph names, completing the three the plan's own text promised. `harness/skills/console/scripts/console.py` gains `section_needs_your_eye(vault)`, an honest-dark section reading the identical `_meta/needs-your-eye.json` task 3 already writes: dark (no file yet â€” the weekly cycle hasn't run on this machine since the list shipped), "nothing" (an empty list â€” every candidate resolved), or a bulleted list of ambiguous pairs by file name and reason. Both readers are best-effort: a missing or malformed file returns zero/dark rather than raising, the same honest-dark convention every other console section and brief signal already follows. This also resolves the [Autonomy design](agentm-autonomy.md#delivery--getting-it-in-front-of-you)'s own forward reference â€” its Session-start line bullet named the needs-your-eye count as reading only "the observability layer's own live signal... until the auto-organization needs-your-eye list ships and widens it"; the brief's count now reads both signals side by side (parked runs, then dedup candidates), additive rather than a replacement â€” see that design's own amendment log for the matching entry. **Tests (task 5):** `scripts/test_needs_your_eye_surface.py`, 3 new â€” a fixture ambiguous pair appears on all three surfaces (digest, console, brief count) after one triage cycle; resolving it (editing one note apart) clears all three on the next cycle; the console section renders honest-dark before any cycle has run.

The backlog drains like the link debt does: each cycle collapses a capped batch of the existing suffix families until none remain.

**As built (task 3 â€” one scope boundary):** pass 1 today collapses every qualifying exact family it finds in the inbox pool in a single run, with no batch cap of its own â€” the capped, cursor-tracked drain this paragraph describes is task 6's job, still pending, not something this task already does by having no cap today.

**As built (task 6 â€” the suffix-backlog drain):** shipped as `dream._stage_suffix_backlog_drain(vault_path, entries, loaded)` (a new `_SUFFIX_BACKLOG_BATCH_CAP = 25` constant alongside it, matching the cap every other batched stage in this design uses), wired into `run_dream()`'s proposal list right after the link-improvement stage. The stage groups every `status: active` entry outside `personal/_always-load/` (curated notes are never a collapse candidate here, same as everywhere else in this design) by `dedup_guard.live_content_fingerprint` â€” the identical live-recomputed hash the write-time guard and task 3's inbox pass already match candidates against, so a suffix family here means the same thing it means everywhere else in this design: content-identical modulo formatting. Linking also improves over time, cheaply. Each weekly cycle, a link-improvement sweep takes the notes that arrived or changed since the last cycle and asks two free questions of the graph snapshot and the vector index: which older notes sit near this one, and which clusters does it touch. Clear candidates get their links added directly â€” both directions, capped, revert-logged. Ambiguous candidates â€” similarity in the middle band â€” go to a cheap model for a yes or no, under a small per-cycle budget declared in the job manifest. When the model tier is unavailable or the budget is spent, the sweep keeps the deterministic links and moves on. Links are only ever added; the single removal case belongs to lint, and it is a link whose target is gone.

**As built (task 4 â€” the weekly link-improvement sweep):** the sweep is real and shipped, but the cheap-model band this paragraph names for the ambiguous middle band was never built. Research done before this task started confirmed no synchronous "ask a cheap model X, get yes or no back, capped by a budget" primitive exists anywhere in this codebase â€” the job manifest's `budget_tokens` field is parsed and never consumed â€” and every prior instance of this exact problem in this codebase (a cron or hook context needing LLM judgment: `adapt_skills.py`'s deterministic-then-sub-agent split, `orchestration_idle.py`'s explicit note that a hook fires outside the agent loop and cannot dispatch one, `forward_learning.py`'s deliberately deterministic-only v1) resolved it by staying deterministic rather than building a new primitive. With the operator's sign-off, the sweep ships deterministic-only: a new `dream.cheap_model_tier_available()` function (always returns `False`) is the explicit, testable seam a future build wires a real budget-and-call primitive to. Until then, every ambiguous-band candidate â€” similarity between `write_time_linker.LINK_SIMILARITY_FLOOR` (0.70) and `CONFIDENT_SIMILARITY_THRESHOLD` (0.85) â€” falls through to "left unlinked," exactly the outcome this paragraph already names for "budget exhausted or tier unavailable," just unconditionally today rather than conditionally on a budget that has nothing to spend from yet.

`link_improvement` also joined `AUTO_APPLY_STAGES` alongside `compression` and `tidying` (`dream_confirm.py`), auto-applying without an operator `confirm()` call. That rests on this same design's own already-approved words above â€” "Every shelf, archive, and link move auto-appliesâ€¦ none of it waits on you" â€” together with the plan's own Locked design call, "Additive only. No link removal happens in this part, ever." Both together are the fresh, separate operator ruling `AUTO_APPLY_STAGES`'s own docstring requires before a new stage joins it.

The sweep runs on its own dedicated cursor (`_read_link_sweep_cursor` / `_write_link_sweep_cursor`, backed by `_meta/link-sweep-cursor.json`), deliberately not task 2's `graph_snapshot.rebuild()` `touched_paths`. An adversarial review caught a real bug in an earlier version that reused that signal: the write-time linker (task 3) already calls `graph_snapshot.rebuild(vault, paths=[rel_path])` for every note it links, so any note that had already picked up an ordinary write-time link â€” the common case, and precisely the notes most likely to have qualifying neighbors â€” had its "changed" signal silently consumed before the weekly sweep ever ran. The dedicated cursor fixed it.

Clear candidates apply through `write_time_linker.merge_related_slugs()`, a new pure helper shared with task 3's `apply()`. It is fence-aware (it will not mutate a `**Related:**`-shaped line sitting inside a fenced code example) and last-match-wins (it will not silently drop one of two duplicate Related lines) â€” both bugs an adversarial review caught and fixed before this shipped. Proposals cap at `_LINK_IMPROVEMENT_BATCH_CAP` (25) per cycle, matching compression's own cap. There is no anomaly breaker scoped to this stage the way `check_tidying_anomaly` guards tidying specifically (see the "As built (part 1)" note below) â€” a fixed cap is the only safeguard today, a known and deliberately deferred gap, not an oversight.

#### Tidying on age â€” artifacts to the shelf, memories to the archive

*Part 1, shipped 2026-07-18 â€” the section below describes what actually built; see [Experience & Dreaming](agentm-experience-and-dreaming.md#dreaming--designed-and-built-dry-run--opt-in) for the pipeline-level record.*

Tidying treats work artifacts and memories differently, and both lanes are automatic.

Work artifacts â€” plans, project files, and the other non-memory documents â€” leave the eyeline two ways. Project artifacts tidy at close-out: the dev workflow already archives a finished plan and buries a finished project after harvest, and this design composes those conventions as they stand. Everything else moves to its tier's `_shelf/` after a full year of never entering a conversation. A shelved artifact changes in no other way: everyday search still finds it, and one use brings it back to its folder on the next cycle. The shelf is a browse convention, not a search boundary â€” the one small engine change is that the recall walker includes `_shelf/`.

As built, "an artifact" is any corpus entry with no `kind:` frontmatter field at all â€” a real memory's `kind:` is a required field in `save.py`'s locked entry contract, so its absence is what marks something as "the other non-memory documents" this section means, not a second, separately-invented classification. "Touch" reuses the exact mechanism the memory lane already tracks â€” a genuine `recall.py` hit â€” rather than a new "any injection into a conversation" tracker: nothing in this codebase implements that broader sense yet, and building it would mean instrumenting skills and hooks that live in the separate `crickets` repo, disproportionate to this one part. The design's own words above name the fuller intent; this is the scoped, buildable slice of it that shipped.

Memories skip the shelf and simply fade. A memory holds full strength for six months of silence, ranks at half strength to a year, an eighth to three years, and a sixteenth to five. Notes entering the final approach â€” past four and a half years of silence â€” appear in the digest one cycle before they move, a preview rather than a gate. Past five years without a single genuine recall, a memory moves to its tier's `_archive/` â€” where it is finally allowed to be forgotten. An archived memory leaves everyday search, stays in the index, and answers an explicit archive search (`--include-archive`) whenever you ask. Nothing is ever deleted, and one genuine recall at any point resets the clock to zero.

Every move in both lanes auto-applies, lands in the revert log, and shows in the digest.

#### Lint

The lint engine walks the corpus and reports four things. Orphans are notes with no links in either direction. They feed the linker's backfill queue. Broken wikilinks get repaired when the fix is safe. A safe fix is a mis-cased target that gets corrected and revert-logged. The engine surfaces unsafe fixes for you to review. Removal has exactly one case â€” a broken link; a valid link is never removed. Contradictions include supersedes chains that disagree and status fields that conflict. The engine surfaces these for your manual resolution. Every note gets a quality score based on frontmatter completeness, link presence, and staleness. A vault-wide roll-up of these scores lands in the digest. The engine also flags `kind:` values that sit outside the registry. This list gives the standing canonicalization cleanup its worklist. You can run `/memory lint` on demand. Dreaming runs it weekly.

**As built (task 7 â€” the lint engine):** shipped as a new `harness/skills/memory/scripts/lint.py`, composing three existing signals into one report rather than building a fourth: `vault_lint.py`'s structural check suite (which itself gains four checks this same task adds â€” see below), `graph_snapshot.orphans()` for the orphan list (already the pool `dream.py`'s own link-improvement backfill drains directly; lint reports the identical signal for visibility, it doesn't create a second feed), and a new per-note quality score â€” a weighted composite of frontmatter completeness (0.4, from `frontmatter_validator.validate()`'s violation count), link presence (0.3, binary â€” orphan-set membership), and staleness (0.3, `lifecycle.compute_decay_score`'s own axis) â€” rolled into a vault-wide mean that lands in `run_dream()`'s digest as a new "Lint:" line alongside the connectivity meter.

The plan's own scoped auto-repair â€” a mis-cased `[[Wrong-Case]]` target where `[[Correct-Case]]` is the sole vault-wide case-insensitive match, no alias, no anchor â€” is the one behavior beyond reporting. It applies via a precise span-based replacement (never a whole-file string `.replace()`) and skips anything inside a fenced code block, reusing `write_time_linker`'s own fence-exclusion helpers. Everything else stays surfaced-only exactly as the plan's own "anything unsafe is surfaced instead" text requires: a genuinely broken link (no unique case-insensitive match), an aliased or anchored occurrence, and the three new contradiction checks `vault_lint.py` gained alongside this task â€” `check_supersede_cycle` (a supersedes chain looping back on itself), `check_supersede_fork` (two or more entries both claiming to supersede the same target), and `check_dangling_supersession_status` (`status: superseded` with no entry's `supersedes:` pointing at it) â€” plus `check_kind_taxonomy`, which wraps `kind_registry.is_known()` directly per this plan's own Constraints text rather than reimplementing the check. `kind_registry.py`'s `KNOWN_KINDS` gained `"howto"` as a direct result: the first check to actually enforce the registry against a live vault found this repo's own `scripts/verify-memory-roundtrip.sh` CI fixture using it, unregistered since the 2026-07-10 seed â€” a real pre-existing gap fixed by registering the kind, not by weakening the new check.

`dream.py`'s `_stage_lint()` calls `lint.run_lint(vault_path)` as a genuinely standalone scan â€” deliberately not threaded through `run_dream()`'s own `entries`/`loaded` snapshot â€” so the weekly stage and the on-demand CLI verb run the identical code path and can't drift, satisfying the plan's own CLI/weekly-parity verification requirement by construction rather than by a separate check. `"lint"` joined `dream_confirm.AUTO_APPLY_STAGES`, scoped narrowly: the stage only ever proposes `kind="wikilink_repair"` mutations, so joining the auto-apply set can never reach any of lint's other findings.

**One design invariant this task did not build:** the design's own Overview ("the single removal case is a link that is already broken") and this section's own text above ("Removal has exactly one case â€” a broken link; a valid link is never removed") both describe lint actually deleting a broken link whose target no longer exists. That never shipped â€” a genuinely broken link stays a surfaced finding, exactly like every other unsafe case, and nothing in `lint.py` or `vault_lint.py` removes a link. The plan's own task 7 text already scoped this narrower from the start (auto-correct-if-mis-cased, else surface â€” no removal clause), and neither of the two tasks left in this plan (8, the meters; 9, the remaining guards) picks it up. This stays designed-for, unscheduled. *Re-audit trigger: revisit this note if a later task or plan actually builds broken-link removal â€” reconcile the Overview and this section's prose against what shipped rather than leaving them read as already-built.*

**Adversarial review caught two real defects before this landed, both fixed same-session.** First, an early version repaired a mis-cased link with a whole-file string `.replace()`, which could silently rewrite a wikilink shown as a documentation example inside a fenced code block â€” fixed by switching to the span-based replacement above, with fence-exclusion reusing `write_time_linker._fenced_ranges`/`_in_any_range`, the identical pattern the link-improvement sweep already relies on. Second, the finding-suppression step (dropping a "broken link" error once its mis-cased occurrence auto-repairs) used a boolean "any match" check that could wrongly suppress an unrelated, still-broken finding â€” an aliased occurrence of the same wrong-cased target (deliberately never auto-repaired, since aliases are skipped for safety) renders an identical message to the repaired bare occurrence, because `vault_lint.check_wikilinks` strips the alias before rendering. Fixed with count-based suppression: only as many findings are dropped per `(entry, wrong-target)` pair as there are actual repaired occurrences. #### Guarding the automation

Four guards keep the automation honest. A sampled audit runs on a higher model tier: each period it takes a random sample of the cycle's applied links and merges and renders agree or disagree. The disagreement rate is a meter; past a threshold, the ambiguous bands narrow toward deterministic-only on their own, and the console flags the change. A global mutation budget bounds each cycle across every stage combined, on top of the per-stage caps. An anomaly breaker watches the trailing average: a cycle that proposes several times the usual mutation count applies nothing at all and flags the console instead. And lint checks the graph snapshot against a live extraction sample each cycle, since three features now lean on it.

**As built (part 1, updated task 4):** the global mutation budget and the anomaly breaker exist today, scoped to the tidying stage's own moves â€” `dream_confirm.auto_apply_batch`'s `batch_cap` already bounds every auto-apply stage combined (compression, tidying, and â€” as of task 4 â€” `link_improvement` together, since it joined `AUTO_APPLY_STAGES` too), and `dream_confirm.check_tidying_anomaly` trips when a cycle's tidying-proposal count exceeds three times a trailing eight-cycle baseline, applying nothing from that stage rather than a capped partial batch. `link_improvement` has no equivalent anomaly breaker of its own yet â€” only its fixed `_LINK_IMPROVEMENT_BATCH_CAP` (25) per-cycle cap, named in its own as-built note above â€” a known, deliberately deferred gap that task 9 (below) leaves exactly as-is by design, scoping its own generalization to this part's own stages. The sampled higher-tier audit and the fuller guard suite over dedup/lint land with part 3, task 9 â€” see the three as-built notes immediately below.

**As built (task 6 â€” the cross-stage auto-apply collision guard):** adversarial review of this task surfaced a real bug that predates it and isn't scoped to dedup at all â€” this is simply the first time two auto-apply stages could plausibly collide, since `suffix_backlog_drain` is the second stage (after `tidying`) capable of proposing a mutation against a note the same cycle another stage also touches. Both stages compute their proposals against one frozen pre-cycle snapshot with no awareness of each other; applying both blindly â€” confirmed by a reproduction â€” could resurrect a note tidying had just archived, back at its old path, carrying the stale frontmatter tidying's own move had already superseded. The fix lives in `dream_confirm.auto_apply_batch` (its own docstring carries the full account): the apply loop now tracks every path an already-applied proposal in the current batch has touched, and skips â€” leaves pending, doesn't consume a `batch_cap` slot â€” any later proposal whose paths overlap one already applied this cycle. A skipped proposal is re-evaluated fresh on a later cycle, once the earlier change has settled, by which point the relevant stage's own scan either no longer sees the path as a candidate or proposes a fresh, correctly based mutation against it. This closes a gap the guards above don't otherwise cover: the per-stage caps and the global mutation budget bound how much any ONE stage moves in a cycle, but nothing before this fix bounded two DIFFERENT stages acting on the same note in the same cycle. The guard is general â€” it protects any future pair of auto-apply stages, not just this one. Verified end-to-end by `CrossStageAutoApplyCollisionTests` in `scripts/test_dream.py` (reproducing the real tidying/suffix-backlog-drain collision through the actual `run_dream_and_auto_apply()` pipeline), plus 3 focused unit tests in `scripts/test_dream_confirm.py::CrossStageCollisionGuardTests` exercising the skip logic directly.

**As built (task 9 of 9 â€” the generalized anomaly breaker).** `dream_confirm.check_tidying_anomaly` â€” tidying-only since part 1 â€” generalized into `dream_confirm.check_stage_anomaly(vault_path, stage, current_count)`, taking the stage name as a parameter and reading/writing a separate `_meta/<stage>-cycle-history.json` sidecar per stage, so one stage's spike can never trip or poison another's baseline. `check_tidying_anomaly` itself survives as a thin `stage="tidying"` wrapper over the generalized function â€” byte-identical behavior, same history-file path, no existing call site or test needed to change. `dream.run_dream_and_auto_apply()` now runs every stage named in a new `_ANOMALY_WATCHED_STAGES = ("tidying", "suffix_backlog_drain", "lint")` tuple through its own `check_stage_anomaly` call before applying anything, excluding any tripped stage from that cycle's auto-apply set while leaving every other stage unaffected â€” the digest carries one flagged section per tripped stage (`_render_digest`'s old `tidying_anomaly` parameter widened into `anomalies: dict[str, AnomalyCheckResult]`, one section rendered per tripped entry) and `_meta/dream-anomaly-latest.json` changed shape from a single flat dict to a list of per-stage records, one per stage that tripped that cycle. **`link_improvement` is deliberately NOT in `_ANOMALY_WATCHED_STAGES`** â€” this task's own plan text scopes the extension to "this part's own dedup/lint mutations," not part 2's linking stage, so the gap this design's own task-4 note above already names (link_improvement has only its fixed per-cycle cap, no anomaly breaker) stays exactly as documented, not silently widened. Verified by `StageAnomalyGeneralizationTests` (`scripts/test_dream_confirm.py`, 3 tests: the generalized function trips/doesn't-trip/records history identically to the old tidying-only one, and `check_tidying_anomaly` is proven to be the thin wrapper) and `MultiStageAnomalyBreakerIntegrationTests` (`scripts/test_dream.py`, 1 test: a real `suffix_backlog_drain` spike trips independently of tidying through the actual `run_dream_and_auto_apply()` pipeline, tidying's own baseline untouched).

**As built (task 9 of 9 â€” the graph-snapshot cross-check).** New `lint._graph_snapshot_cross_check(vault_path, model)` compares each sampled entry's PERSISTED outgoing edges (`graph_snapshot.outgoing()`, part 2's own snapshot) against a LIVE re-extraction from its current content (`graph.extract_edges_for_paths()`), flagging any disagreement as a `graph-snapshot-drift` finding. **Ordering is load-bearing and required a `run_dream()` reorder beyond this task's own module:** `lint.run_lint()`'s own `graph_snapshot.rebuild()` call reconciles the whole vault against live content as a side effect of running lint at all, so the cross-check has to run and capture its findings BEFORE that rebuild â€” otherwise it would compare a freshly-rebuilt snapshot against itself moments later and never find anything. Wiring the cross-check into the weekly cycle surfaced a second rebuild call that would have broken the same invariant even with `lint.py`'s own internal ordering fixed: `dream.py`'s `_stage_link_improvement` (part 2, task 4) also does an unconditional full-vault `graph_snapshot.rebuild()` as its own side effect, and `run_dream()`'s hand-wired stage sequence ran link-improvement before lint. Fixed by reordering `run_dream()` so `_stage_lint()` runs FIRST, before every other stage â€” the only way the cross-check sees genuine week-old drift rather than a snapshot no other stage has touched yet this cycle.

The batch itself needed a real fix, caught by adversarial review of the first version: the sample was a plain `sorted(all_rels)[:cap]` slice with no persisted rotation, so any entry past the alphabetically-first 25 was PERMANENTLY unreachable no matter how many cycles ran â€” a starvation bug, not a coverage gap that would eventually self-correct. Fixed with a persisted rotation cursor (`_read_cross_check_attempted` / `_write_cross_check_attempted`, backed by `_meta/graph-snapshot-cross-check-state.json`), mirroring `dream._read_backfill_attempted`'s identical pool-rotation shape from part 2's link-backfill: each cycle samples up to `_GRAPH_SNAPSHOT_CROSS_CHECK_BATCH_CAP` (25) entries the current pass hasn't attempted yet, and once every entry has had a turn, the pass resets and starts over. **This is a general lesson worth carrying into any future batched check in this design or elsewhere: a capped batch drawn from a fixed sort-and-slice, with no persisted cursor, silently starves everything past the cap forever â€” the fix is always a persisted rotation cursor, not a bigger cap or a smarter sort.** Deterministic order within a pass (sorted rel path), not one true-random sample per cycle, matching the design's own capped-batch convention elsewhere rather than the literal word "sample." Verified by `GraphSnapshotCrossCheckTests` (`scripts/test_lint.py`, 6 tests, including two regression tests pinned directly to the rotation fix: a fixture with more than 25 entries proves every entry eventually gets checked across successive cycles, and a fixture proves the same entry is never checked twice in one pass before the rest of the pool has had a turn).

**As built (task 9 of 9 â€” the sampled higher-tier audit and band narrowing).** New `dream_confirm.higher_tier_model_available()` â€” always `False`, for the identical reason `dream.cheap_model_tier_available()` (part 2, task 4) is: research before this task confirmed no synchronous "ask a model X, get a verdict back" primitive exists anywhere in this codebase, the same finding, same seam shape, same fail-closed contract as every other consumer of that pattern in this design. `judge_applied_mutation(summary)` raises if ever called â€” unreachable until the tier ships, never a silent guess. `run_sampled_audit(vault_path, applied_items)` samples up to `SAMPLED_AUDIT_BATCH_CAP` (25) of the cycle's applied items, deterministically (sorted by index, draining coverage over successive cycles the same way the graph-snapshot cross-check above does â€” not one true-random draw per run, despite this section's own "random sample" wording above), asks the higher tier for a verdict on each, and computes a disagreement rate. Above `SAMPLED_AUDIT_DISAGREEMENT_THRESHOLD` (0.2, i.e. >20%), it narrows the ambiguous band by raising `write_time_linker`'s own similarity floor: a persisted override (`_meta/link-band-narrowing.json`) is written with the floor stepped up by a fixed `_LINK_BAND_NARROWING_STEP` (0.05), capped just below `CONFIDENT_SIMILARITY_THRESHOLD` (0.85) so narrowing can approach but never cross into the already-confident band. New `write_time_linker.link_similarity_floor(vault_path=None)` is the reader every consumer now calls instead of the raw `LINK_SIMILARITY_FLOOR` constant â€” it falls back to that constant when no override file exists â€” and `dream.py`'s `_stage_link_improvement` (part 2) was updated to call it, so a narrowing this task writes actually changes the weekly sweep's real behavior, not just a number nobody reads.

`dream.run_dream_and_auto_apply()` gathers the cycle's actual applied `link_improvement` items from the real `batch.items`, plus the folded inbox-triage sub-run's applied `inbox_merge` items from `triage_batch.items` â€” "links and merges," matching this section's own wording â€” and passes both into `run_sampled_audit`. The result lands on a new `DreamDigest.sampled_audit` field, is written to `_meta/sampled-audit-latest.json` (overwritten every cycle, the same "never goes stale" convention as `dream-auto-expired-latest.json`), and renders as a "Sampled audit:" line in the digest. New `console.section_sampled_audit(vault)` reads that same pointer file â€” honest-dark before any cycle has run, "nothing sampled this cycle" (not an error) while the tier stays unavailable, and a `âš  ambiguous bands narrowed this cycle` flag appended to the line whenever a narrowing fires â€” this is the "flagging the console" this section's own opening paragraph requires.

**Because the higher-tier model tier is always unavailable, every REAL cycle samples nothing, records no disagreement, and never narrows** â€” a named, tracked gap exactly like every other consumer of this codebase's model-tier seam (`dream.cheap_model_tier_available()`, part 2 task 4; `dream_confirm.higher_tier_model_available()`, here), not a silently-dropped feature. Tests exercise the full sampling/disagreement-rate/narrowing logic end-to-end by patching `higher_tier_model_available` to `True` and supplying verdicts â€” the same convention `dream.py`'s `judge_fuzzy_merge` tests (part 3, task 3) already established. Verified by `SampledAuditTests` (`scripts/test_dream_confirm.py`, 6 tests, including a proof that a narrowing above threshold actually writes the directive file and a proof that successive narrowings never cross the `CONFIDENT_SIMILARITY_THRESHOLD` cap no matter how many cycles trip in a row), `test_narrowed_floor_excludes_a_pair_that_would_otherwise_qualify` (`scripts/test_dream.py::LinkImprovementStageTests` â€” proving `_stage_link_improvement` actually reads the narrowed floor and excludes a pair it would otherwise have linked, not just that the reader function works in isolation), `test_sampled_audit_gathers_a_real_applied_link_improvement_item` (`scripts/test_dream.py::LinkImprovementIntegrationTests` â€” closing a coverage gap adversarial review flagged: proving the real `batch.items` dict shape from an actual applied link-improvement proposal flows correctly into `run_sampled_audit`, not just a synthetic dict shaped to match), and the standalone `scripts/test_sampled_audit_surface.py::SampledAuditConsoleSurfaceTests` (4 tests, directly proving the plan's own verification text â€” the console section renders honest-dark before any cycle, reports "nothing" honestly at zero samples, and is flagged exactly when the bands narrow).

#### The meters

Two numbers say whether this design is working. Organic connectivity starts at 13.8%. This is the share of notes with at least one real, non-generated link. The meter counts the linker's additions separately. This separate count keeps the main number honest. The browse-surface counts track the eyeline directly. They count three states: live notes per folder, shelved, and archived. Both metrics land in the digest each cycle. ## Alternatives Considered

- **A dedicated nightly runner job for archival.** Rejected: Dreaming owns whole-corpus maintenance. It already handles staging, confirmation, reverting, and auto-apply. A second corpus walker duplicates this work. A weekly schedule is fast enough. Nothing about a cold note requires an urgent response.
- **LLM-judged linking and archiving.** Rejected for v1: Deterministic signals like similarity, decay, and the graph are cheap and explainable. The engine already computes them. An LLM judge can act as a second opinion later if the deterministic signal is uncertain.
- **Hard-deleting expired cruft.** Rejected outright: Prune always resolves to archive. It never resolves to delete. We maintain this standing invariant.
- **Obsidian community plugins for auto-linking.** Rejected: The engine owns the corpus. It works the exact same way on every host. A plugin forks the mechanism per host and skips the staging discipline.
- **Do nothing.** Rejected: Connectivity is flat. The browse surface degrades every month. You named this pain directly.

## Technical Debt & Risks

- The stepped rank schedule, the one-year artifact bar, the five-year memory bar, the similarity floor, and the link cap are calibration defaults. They deliberately lean conservative, and they have no real-use data behind them. *Re-audit trigger:* The first two live cycles.
- Retuning the rank curve changes retrieval order vault-wide. The change merges only if the pinned retrieval eval holds â€” the standing rule for any ranking change.
- Over-linking is the linker's failure mode. Too many weak links make the graph noisy. The cap and the floor bound this behavior. The connectivity meter counts generated links separately. This prevents the linker from inflating its own success number.
- Shelving an artifact you still want in the eyeline is the likeliest miss, and it is low-stakes: it stays in everyday search, the digest names every move, the revert log undoes it, and one use brings it back. Archiving a memory wrongly is rarer still at a five-year bar, and the archive search plus the revert log recover it.
- Contradiction detection is heuristic and advisory. It misses real contradictions and flags false ones. It only surfaces problems and never acts on them.
- The backfill touches old notes at scale. The per-cycle cap and the revert log bound the blast radius for each cycle.
- The cheap-model band could add weak links or run up cost. The band is narrow, the additions are capped and counted separately by the meter, the job manifest carries a hard per-cycle budget, and the sweep degrades to deterministic-only when the budget is spent or the tier is unavailable. The sampled higher-tier audit measures its judgment, and the bands narrow on their own when disagreement climbs.
- A runaway cycle could churn the vault inside the per-stage caps. The global mutation budget and the anomaly breaker bound it: an abnormal cycle applies nothing and flags the console.
- A wrong merge is dedup's failure mode. A collapse keeps the surviving note's content and marks the copies superseded rather than deleting anything, every disposition is revert-logged and named in the digest, and the per-cycle cap bounds each pass. Ambiguous candidates are never forced: they stay in the inbox and go on the needs-your-eye list. *Re-audit trigger:* the first backlog-drain cycles.

## Quality Attributes

### Reliability

The engine caps and revert-logs every scheduled action, and reports each one in the digest. Every action is resumable. A cycle that moves nothing is a normal cycle.

### Data Integrity

Shelf and archive are moves, never deletes. The index covers both, so nothing becomes unfindable. The engine journals every mutation for revert.

### Latency

The linker runs asynchronously after the save returns. It works the same way embedding does. Saving a note never waits on the linker. The weekly cycle carries everything else.

### Testability

Each action gets a deterministic fixture. The linker uses a synthetic corpus with known neighbors. The archive stage uses notes with pinned decay scores. Lint uses seeded rot. The connectivity meter has its own test. The supervised first passes over the real vault provide the acceptance evidence.
